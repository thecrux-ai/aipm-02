# TalentScope — User Research Transcripts

**Context:** Three user research interviews conducted by TalentScope's PM team with HR managers at Indian mid-market companies. The goal was to understand hiring pain points before building the AI screening product.

---

## Interview 1

**Participant:** Anita Verma, Head of HR, Bharat Forge (Pune) — 1,800 employees, manufacturing
**Interview date:** April 2026
**Duration:** 42 minutes

---

**Interviewer:** Walk me through how you hire for a standard Associate-level role right now. Start from when the role opens.

**Anita:** So we get the role requisition from the business head — usually a form, sometimes just a WhatsApp message saying "Anita, we need two more people in the quality team." Then I post on Naukri and LinkedIn. Within 48 hours we're drowning. For anything junior we get 400-600 applications. For senior roles it's less, maybe 80-120, but those are harder to evaluate.

My team is three people. We divide the applications. Each of us is doing maybe 150 resumes. And honestly, the first pass is — does this person have the minimum experience? If yes, we phone screen. If no, we reject. We're not doing a nuanced evaluation at that volume. It's pattern matching.

**Interviewer:** What does that pattern matching look like?

**Anita:** Domain keywords, years of experience, last company. If someone worked at a reputable company in the same industry, they go to the phone screen pile. If they're from a completely different industry, they usually get rejected unless something jumps out. We know this is imperfect. We've missed people, I'm sure.

**Interviewer:** How long does a phone screen take?

**Anita:** 15-20 minutes. We're trying to check three things: communication skills, motivation for the role, and whether they understand what we do. Most of the time by 10 minutes I know. But scheduling is the real killer. We phone screen 30-40 people for a role. Getting them to pick up, reschedule, coordinate — that's where my team's time goes. Literally 3-4 hours of coordination per role.

**Interviewer:** What happens after the phone screen?

**Anita:** I create a shortlist of 8-10 and share with the hiring manager. They pick 4-5 for a technical interview. Then another round with the department head. Then HR round and offer. Total process is 4-6 weeks for most roles.

**Interviewer:** What's the failure mode you most want to avoid?

**Anita:** Two. One: missing a good candidate in the first pass because the resume doesn't show what the person can actually do. We hire a lot of people who are good at their job but not at writing resumes. Two: wasting a hiring manager's time with a wrong fit in the technical round. Every time I send someone to the hiring manager and they're clearly not qualified, I lose credibility. And hiring managers are already skeptical of HR — they think we don't understand the technical requirements.

**Interviewer:** If you could change one part of the process, what would it be?

**Anita:** The first pass. I want to spend less time on it and I want it to be less arbitrary. Right now my junior HR person is doing the first pass for 150 resumes and making judgment calls based on a two-minute skim. I know that's not great but I don't have another option at this volume. If something could do a more thoughtful first pass and explain why it shortlisted or rejected someone, I'd trust it more than what we do today.

**Interviewer:** What would "more thoughtful" look like to you?

**Anita:** It would tell me specifically why it thinks the person is a fit or not — not just a score. I can't defend a score to a hiring manager. I can defend "this candidate has 4 years of quality audit experience in auto components, which matches your requirement, but has never managed a team, which was your secondary requirement." That I can use.

## Interview 2

**Participant:** Rajesh Menon, HR Manager, Sundaram Finance (Chennai) — 5,000 employees, NBFC/financial services
**Interview date:** April 2026
**Duration:** 38 minutes

---

**Interviewer:** How many open roles are you running at any given time?

**Rajesh:** Right now, 22. We're always hiring — attrition in financial services in Chennai is high, especially at the relationship manager and collections level. Those roles are continuous. We hire maybe 400 people a year.

**Interviewer:** What's the biggest bottleneck in your hiring process?

**Rajesh:** Two things. First, the speed. My turnaround commitment to business is 30 days from requisition to offer for standard roles. We're hitting 45-50 days on average. The delay is mostly in the first two stages — screening and first interview. Second, the quality of shortlists I give to business. They're not happy with what they see.

**Interviewer:** Why aren't they happy?

**Rajesh:** We're optimising for speed at the first-pass stage. Fast means imprecise. Someone gets through because their resume looks okay but they haven't actually done the job we need. The business round reveals this, and then they come back to me: "Rajesh, this person doesn't have field experience." I knew that risk — I was trying to show a full shortlist. But it hurts.

**Interviewer:** For relationship manager roles specifically, what are the criteria you're screening for?

**Rajesh:** Core is loan sales experience — at least 2 years in any financial product. Vehicle loans, home loans, personal loans — we take any of those. Then geography: they must have worked in the same zone, ideally the same district. Our customers are very local. Someone from a Trichy branch doesn't know the Chennai customer. Then communication: they need to be confident in Tamil with customers but functional in English for internal reports. Last: no recent job hopping — if they've switched 3 companies in 2 years, that's a flag for us.

**Interviewer:** How do you check these on a resume?

**Rajesh:** Product experience and geography — usually on the resume. Communication — only on a call. Job hopping — you can calculate it from the dates. The problem is our HR team is doing this for 150 resumes per role. For experienced folks, maybe, they can spot the patterns. For my junior people, they miss things. Geography especially — they don't always know which districts are relevant.

**Interviewer:** Would you trust an AI to do this first-pass screening?

**Rajesh:** Conditionally. I would trust it to flag: has financial services experience, tick. Works in Chennai zone, tick. No job hopping, tick. What I would not trust it to do is override my team's judgment on things that need context. There are cases where a candidate switched companies fast but for a good reason — company shutdown, transfer for personal reasons. An AI wouldn't know that.

**Interviewer:** So what's the right role for AI in your process?

**Rajesh:** First pass only. And it must give me a reason, not a score. If it says reject, it must tell me exactly why. Because my team will override rejections if the reason doesn't make sense — we've done it manually, we'll do it with an AI too. I need to be able to explain to a candidate, if they ask, why they weren't shortlisted. "The system rejected you" is not an answer I'm willing to give.

## Interview 3

**Participant:** Pooja Kulkarni, Talent Acquisition Lead, Marico Limited (Mumbai) — 3,500 employees, FMCG
**Interview date:** May 2026
**Duration:** 51 minutes

---

**Interviewer:** Tell me about your biggest hiring challenge right now.

**Pooja:** We're a consumer goods company trying to hire people who are both marketing-savvy and analytically strong. That's a rare combination. Our job descriptions ask for both, but the market mostly offers one or the other. So we're constantly making judgment calls: this person has strong analytics but their marketing instinct seems thin — do we take the bet?

We also hire a lot at the MBA fresher level. For that segment, everyone on paper looks the same. 800-1000 applications. Same tier colleges, similar internship profiles, similar scores. Differentiating on a resume is almost impossible.

**Interviewer:** How do you differentiate then?

**Pooja:** We do group activities and case studies. But those happen later in the process, after the first shortlist. The question is how to get to a shortlist of 50 from 1000 that isn't just arbitrary.

Right now we filter on college tier first. That's our blunt instrument. IIM, XLRI, SPJIMR, FMS — those go into the shortlist automatically. Everything below that we read manually, and honestly, my team doesn't have the bandwidth to read 900 resumes carefully. We're doing 30-second passes.

**Interviewer:** Do you think you're missing people with the college-tier filter?

**Pooja:** Definitely. We've hired people from Tier 2 colleges who are in our top quartile performers. The problem is finding them in 1000 applications. We'd need to read every resume carefully to find those people, and we can't do that.

I've thought about this a lot. What I actually want is something that can read the resume for substance, not for college name. Can this person demonstrate original thinking? Did they do anything with their internship beyond just completing it? Did they take initiative on any project? These things show up in how people write their resume, but you have to read carefully to see them.

**Interviewer:** Do you think AI could read for substance?

**Pooja:** That's the hope. I've tried using ChatGPT for this. I paste a resume and ask it to tell me if the candidate shows initiative. Sometimes the answers are useful. Mostly they're generic — it says things that could apply to any candidate. The problem is the AI is reading the resume in isolation. It doesn't know what we care about, it doesn't know our culture, it doesn't know what "initiative" looks like in an FMCG context.

**Interviewer:** What would make it useful?

**Pooja:** If I could give it our context — what we value, what success looks like in the role, what we've seen from people who made it vs. people who didn't — and then have it read each resume against that context. Not generic advice. Our specific criteria.

And I want it to surface the ones that it's uncertain about. The easy cases — clearly qualified or clearly not qualified — those I trust a machine to handle. The in-between cases, I want those flagged for my team to review. My team's time is best spent on the decisions that are actually hard, not the ones that are obvious.

**Interviewer:** What would make you not trust the AI's recommendation?

**Pooja:** If it recommended someone and they turned out to be bad. One bad recommendation would make my team skeptical of all of them. Trust is brittle. If the AI gets it wrong in a visible case — recommends someone who embarrasses us in the interview — we'd go back to doing everything manually. That's the risk.
