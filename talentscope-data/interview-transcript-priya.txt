Both, honestly. I've since advocated for putting some of these signals into our actual health scoring system. We did get a couple of them added — login frequency and repeat-ticket-on-same-issue — as factors in our health score. Not a full churn model, but better than nothing.

[INTERVIEW AGENT FOLLOW-UP 3]

That's a realistic framing. Last question: the job description mentions that success in year one includes zero preventable churns. How do you define "preventable" versus a churn that you just couldn't stop?

---

[CANDIDATE RESPONSE 4]

A preventable churn is one where there was a signal I could have acted on and I didn't — either because I didn't notice it, or I noticed and deprioritised it, or I took the wrong action.

An unpreventable churn is one where the signal was absent, or the root cause was outside anything a CSM could address — budget cuts, company shutdown, M&A, a decision made at the CEO level for reasons that have nothing to do with product satisfaction.

The grey zone is churn where there was a signal but the root cause was something we couldn't fix — a product gap, a pricing mismatch, a competitor offering something we don't. In those cases, I think the CSM's job is to have documented the risk early, escalated it internally, and made sure the company understood why we were losing this account. That's not a preventable churn in my view, but it is a learning we have to capture.

I track my churns retrospectively — for every account that left, I write a one-paragraph "churn story" for myself: what happened, when the signal appeared, what I did, what I should have done differently. I've shared these with my team lead as a pattern recognition exercise. Of my 3 churns in 5 years, one was a company shutdown, one was a competitor with a significantly lower price point that we couldn't match, and one is one I replay — there was a signal I saw and deprioritised. That's the one I learn from.
